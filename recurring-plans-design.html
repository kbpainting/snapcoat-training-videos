Recurring Service Plans

Design for report #52 — Greg Chung. Read this before I build anything; one decision changes the whole shape.

"sell maintenance packages for recurring revenue… bill monthly or yearly… have the proposal live in snapcoat, and then have a recurring event on my job schedule every year in the same month for me to assign to a team and generate a work order"

Three separate things in one sentence: a plan you sell, money that repeats, and work that reappears on the calendar. They have very different costs and risks, and they don't have to ship together.

The decision — everything downstream depends on it
The thing nobody's flagged. We already have company_subscriptions, subscription_plans, subscription_invoices, subscription_events. None of it can be reused. All of that bills our customers for SnapCoat through our Stripe account. Greg wants to bill his customers on his processor — which is Helcim. Different money, different direction, different liability. This is genuinely net-new.

Pick one

A — Full auto-billingHighest risk

Card on file, we charge it on a schedule without anyone touching it.

  • Best product. Truly passive revenue, which is what Greg is actually asking for.
  • But: we'd be storing/reusing payment credentials on behalf of our customers, and inheriting failed payments, dunning, expired cards, refunds and chargebacks — a whole support surface we don't have today.
  • Requires confirming Helcim supports stored-card recurring charges per-company, and that we're comfortable holding that.
B — Recurring invoice, customer paysRecommended for v1

On schedule we generate the invoice and send it. The customer pays it through the same flow they already use for any other invoice.

  • Delivers most of the value — the billing happens on its own, nobody has to remember.
  • Reuses the existing invoices table and payment path completely. No card storage, no dunning engine, no new liability.
  • If Greg wants true auto-charge later, A is a strict upgrade on top of this — none of it gets thrown away.
C — Scheduling onlyCheapest

The recurring job lands on the calendar. Billing stays entirely manual.

  • Solves the "so that it is on the radar when that time comes" half, which is the part he described most concretely.
  • Nearly free, and it's a genuine slice of B rather than throwaway work.

My recommendation: B, built so C ships first and standalone. The scheduling half is the part with no risk attached, and it's independently useful the day it lands.

Where it lives

Your instinct — a separate page inside Projects — is right. These aren't jobs; they're agreements that emit jobs. Putting them in the normal pipeline would clog it with rows that never move.

  • Route /projects/recurring, page client/src/pages/projects/recurring.tsx
  • Nav item in client/src/lib/app-navigation.ts alongside Work Orders (production group, legacyVisibility: 'projects', icon RefreshCw)
  • Add /projects/recurring to the /unified-projects prefix list so the hub highlights correctly

The page

  • Active plans — customer, what's included, cadence, price, next service date, next bill date, status
  • Due soon — the next 60 days, the working view: assign a crew, generate the work order, mark done
  • History — every past occurrence with its appointment and invoice, so "when did we last do the Hendersons?" is one glance
Data model
service_plans
  id, company_id, customer_id
  source_proposal_id        -- the proposal that sold it
  name                      -- "Annual Pressure Wash + Touch-Up"
  service_cadence           -- annual | semiannual | quarterly | monthly
  service_anchor_month      -- keeps "same month every year" honest
  billing_cadence           -- monthly | annual | per_service | none
  amount_cents, currency
  next_service_date, next_bill_date
  status                    -- active | paused | cancelled | completed
  auto_generate_work_order  boolean
  created_at, updated_at, cancelled_at, cancel_reason

service_plan_occurrences
  id, plan_id, company_id
  scheduled_date, period_start, period_end
  appointment_id, work_order_id, invoice_id
  status                    -- scheduled | completed | skipped | cancelled
  created_at, completed_at
Why the occurrences table matters more than it looks. It's the audit trail and the idempotency guard. The generator claims a (plan_id, period_start) row before doing anything, so a retry, a double-run, or two workers can never double-bill a customer. Without it, one bad cron night bills everybody twice.
The generator — where this gets dangerous

A scheduled job walks plans whose next_service_date or next_bill_date has arrived, creates the occurrence, then the appointment and/or invoice.

Timezone and time-of-day are not details here. We've been bitten by both. Every date has to be resolved against companies.timezone (all 15 companies have one set) using fromZonedTime — never the server's local time — and the job must never fire overnight in the company's own timezone. A maintenance reminder landing at 3am reads as a system that's broken.
Never send to a real customer without a preview. First release should generate everything in a draft state with a clear "review and send" step. Auto-sending invoices to a contractor's customers on day one is how we'd lose a customer's trust in one bad night. Loosen it once it's proven.

Edge cases that decide the schema — worth answering before I build

CaseQuestion
Cancellation mid-termProrate, honour to the end of the paid period, or stop dead?
Price changeExisting plans grandfathered, or repriced at renewal?
Skipped visitCustomer skips a year — still billed? Does the anchor month shift?
RescheduleMoving one visit — does it move the whole series or just that occurrence?
Failed paymentPause the plan, keep servicing, or flag and continue?
Cadence mismatchGreg's example bills monthly for annual service — so billing and service schedules must be independent. The model above already separates them.

Those last two are the ones that force a rebuild if guessed wrong.

Build order
  1. Schema + the page, read-only. Create plans by hand, see them listed. Proves the model before anything automated exists.
  2. Scheduling generator (option C). Occurrences and appointments only, drafts, no money. Independently shippable and useful on its own.
  3. Work-order generation. Hook into the existing work-order flow from an occurrence.
  4. Billing (option B). Invoice generation on the billing cadence, draft-then-send.
  5. Sell it from a proposal. Flag a proposal as a recurring plan so accepting it creates the plan; the customer-facing document states cadence and terms.
  6. Later, only if wanted: option A auto-charge.
Steps 1–2 are low risk, touch no money, and give Greg the "on my radar" half quickly. Step 4 is the one that needs the Helcim answer, and it doesn't block anything before it.
What I need from you
  • A, B or C? (I'd build toward B, shipping C first.)
  • Does Helcim do stored-card recurring per-company — and do you want us holding that?
  • Cancellation and failed-payment behaviour — the two answers that shape the schema.
  • Is this every plan, or gated to a subscription tier?

Answer those and I'll start on steps 1–2, which I can do without waiting on the billing decision.
